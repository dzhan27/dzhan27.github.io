    Software Engineer @ Cornell Mars Rover

    Sept 2020 - Jun 2023 | Ithaca, New York

    I worked with a team of ~70 undergraduate engineers to design and build a rover to compete in the annual University Rover Challenge. Some tasks these rovers compete in include performing various dexterous operations with the arm and end effector, including inserting USB sticks and typing on keyboards. However, our deprecated ROS 1 Forward-Kinematics based control scheme made it difficult for us to score well on these tasks. To combat this, I implemented an Inverse-Kinematics based control scheme in ROS 2 and C++ to give arm operators direct control over the position and orientation of the end effector, improving our performance in the most complicated tasks. I also re-implemented a Forward-Kinematics based control scheme with more precise joint control as a fallback option during competition.

    Undergrad Research Assistant @ Seyler Group

    Sept 2022 - May 2023 | Ithaca, NY

    I expanded upon 2D and 3D simulations modelling the magnetohydrodynamic flow of ablating plasma. I used Fortran 90 to simulate magnetic fields ablating solid aluminum and manipulating the resulting plasma into a coherent jet. I utilized explicit concurrent programming in the simulation over 32 space cells (corresponding to 32 computational cores available in the simulating computer) with interacting boundaries to reduce simulation runtime from a week to 5 hours.

    Coherent plasma jets have applications to nuclear fusion energy. For example, a basic way to induce nuclear fusion is to point two jets of fusible material in extreme temperature and pressure conditions towards each other. The particulate kinetic energy from the resulting fusion reaction can be collected and used to generate electricity.

    Undergrad Research Assistant @ Fuchs Group

    Sept 2021 - May 2023 | Ithaca, NY

    I developed a computational quantum dynamics model of the NV center. The simulation focuses on quantifying the rate of photoluminescent (PL) transitions. The NV center is excited with a laser, then allowed to relax under varying magnetic field conditions. A near-resonant magnetic field was found to reduce the rate of PL transitions by about 20%. This program is written in Python using the QuTiP (Quantum Toolbox in Python) library.

    The NV center is a point defect in the diamond carbon lattice, where one carbon atom is completely removed and an adjacent carbon atom is replaced with a nitrogen atom. The resulting defect, if negatively charged, has PL transitions. (i.e. You can see when the state of the defect changes, because it emits photons when this happens!) We found that a pulsing magnetic field with frequency near resonance reduces the rate of PL transitions. This could allow us to use the NV center as a quantum sensor; we theoretically could put this point defect somewhere and simply look at it to determine if a magnetic field of that frequency exists there.
